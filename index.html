Read it as a funnel. The era names the period; the threads name the forces that ran through it. Most trace the machinery of racial control — slavery giving way to convict leasing and Jim Crow, then to mass incarceration. Two trace the countercurrents: the rise of Black political power and the building of Black institutions and culture — the wins that were won, stripped away in 1877, and won again. Open any thread and the relationships surface: the 13th Amendment’s “except as a punishment for crime” clause links emancipation to convict leasing and on to mass incarceration; the franchise runs from the 15th Amendment to Shelby County and Callais; and Black political power crests in Reconstruction, collapses, and returns through Marshall, Chisholm, Wilder, Obama, and Jackson. The story is not a steady climb.

On sources & method

Every entry carries a source, favoring primary and archival material: the National Archives for statutes and amendments; the Supreme Court, Oyez, and Justia for rulings (with reporter citations); and institutionally published scholarship for the systems — EJI on racial terror, Rothstein on housing, Katznelson on the New Deal and GI Bill, Blackmon on convict leasing, Alexander on mass incarceration, the Brennan Center on voter suppression. Two threads are flagged where scholars genuinely disagree: the Tulsa death toll, and the causal weight of redlining maps. The "coded politics" system carries a note that reading specific modern phrases as racial code is itself an interpretive, contested lens — its anchor is the primary 1981 Atwater interview and Ian Haney López's scholarship.
